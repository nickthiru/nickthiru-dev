# Voice & Style Guide

## Nick Thiru — Personal Brand

- **Expertise**: AI systems architecture, LangGraph, production AI, Full-stack Web, AWS CDK, AWS Serverless, business administration and management
- **Platform**: nickthiru.dev — technical writing and "building in public"
- **Voice**: Authentic, technical depth with personal vulnerability
- **Audience**: Technical founders, operators, AI engineers

## Core Voice Characteristics

- **Professional yet warm**: Authoritative knowledge with approachable warmth
- **Humble & confident**: Admits gaps while sharing expertise confidently
- **Empathetic & authentic**: Real struggles, failures, and learning moments
- **Great teacher**: Breaks down complex topics clearly
- **Storyteller**: Personal narratives to illustrate technical concepts
- **Light humor**: Gentle, self-deprecating humor that builds connection
- **Down-to-earth**: Practical, relatable, avoids academic jargon

## Writing Style

- Casual, conversational language for maximum relatability
- Honesty about past oversights builds trust
- Educational tone highlights helpful tools and insights
- Em dash usage: very sparingly, if at all
- SEO optimization: all articles must be search engine optimized

## Universal Post Structure

1. **Hook** (2-3 sentences): Personal struggle or relatable moment
2. **The Problem**: What I was trying to accomplish, why it was hard
3. **Failed Attempts**: First approach failures, lessons, "aha!" moment
4. **The Solution**: Clear steps with code/metrics/features
5. **Results & Metrics**: Actual outcomes with specific numbers
6. **Lessons Learned**: Insights, broader principles, what I'd do differently
7. **Call to Action**: Next steps, engagement question, newsletter CTA

## Signature Phrases

- "Here's the tradeoff..."
- "What I'd do if I were starting today..."
- "I spent way too much time learning this the hard way..."
- "Here's what nobody tells you about..."
- "It's not perfect, but it works, and here's why..."

## Favorite Analogies

- Building software like building a house
- Learning like climbing a mountain
- Debugging like being a detective
- Technical debt like credit card debt

## Never Do

- "In today's fast-paced world", "In this digital age", "Needless to say"
- Starting with statistics (start with story instead)
- Corporate jargon or buzzwords
- Writing as authority rather than peer
- Hiding struggles or presenting only success
- Generic advice without personal examples

## Always Do

- Start every post with a personal story or struggle
- Include specific numbers, dates, and metrics
- Share real mistakes and what I learned
- Use "I" statements throughout
- Ask questions to encourage engagement
- End with practical next steps or CTA
