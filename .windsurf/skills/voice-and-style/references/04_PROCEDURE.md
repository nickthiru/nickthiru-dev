# Procedure

## The 4-Step Content Creation Pipeline

Every piece of content follows this pipeline:

```text
Step 1: Ideation & Outline    → Select topic, create structured outline
Step 2: Model Draft            → Write first draft from outline
Step 3: Style Rewrite          → Apply voice, remove jargon, add personality
Step 4: User Edits             → Human review and final adjustments
```

### Step 1: Ideation & Outline

1. Identify the topic from source material (build logs, notes, observations).
2. Classify into content category: engineering, business, or product.
3. Create a structured outline following the category's pattern.

### Step 2: Model Draft

1. Write from the outline.
2. Apply voice characteristics from policies.
3. Include specific numbers, dates, and details from source material.
4. Use teaching style: "why" before "how", real examples, architecture-first.

### Step 3: Style Rewrite

1. Replace any filler phrases or corporate jargon.
2. Ensure story-driven opening (not statistics).
3. Apply signature phrases naturally where they fit.
4. Check humble-yet-confident tone throughout.
5. Add light humor where appropriate.
6. Verify down-to-earth language — no academic jargon.

### Step 4: User Edits

1. Present the draft to the user.
2. The user reviews and makes final adjustments.
3. This is the human quality gate — no content publishes without it.

## Content Categories

### Engineering (~60% trailing target)

Sub-categories: architecture decisions, debugging stories, implementation walkthroughs, performance optimization, tool/framework evaluations, testing strategies.

### Business (~30% trailing target)

Sub-categories: revenue milestones, pricing decisions, market positioning, customer discovery, time management, cost optimization, partnerships.

### Product (~10% trailing target)

Sub-categories: feature launches, user feedback, roadmap decisions, product-market fit experiments, UX decisions.

The percentages are trailing targets reviewed monthly — not forward-planning constraints.

## Teaching Style Application

When explaining technical concepts:

1. **Start with "why"**: Why does this matter? What problem does it solve?
2. **Architecture first**: Show the big picture before diving into code.
3. **Real examples**: Use your actual code, actual errors, actual metrics.
4. **Analogy bridge**: Relate the technical concept to something familiar.
5. **Show the struggle**: Include what you tried that did not work.
6. **End with insight**: What is the broader lesson beyond this specific case?
