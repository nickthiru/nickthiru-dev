## 2. The Old Rules Don't Apply (400 words)

**Thesis:** AI fundamentally changes the leverage equation for software development.

**Traditional SaaS requirements:**

- Engineering team (frontend, backend, DevOps)
- Product management
- Customer support
- Sales and marketing
- Operations and finance

**Why teams were necessary:**

- Complexity required specialization
- 24/7 coverage needed multiple people
- Different skill sets (design, code, ops, sales)
- Workload distribution

**The AI shift:**

- One person can now handle what required 5-10 people
- AI assists with: code generation, documentation, support, content creation
- Automation handles: monitoring, deployment, routine tasks
- Modern tools reduce operational overhead

**Real examples:**

- Pieter Levels: $4M+ ARR solo (NomadList, RemoteOK, PhotoAI)
- Danny Postma: $1M+ ARR solo (Headshot AI, Taplio)
- Marc Louvion: Multiple 6-figure products solo
- Levelsio quote: "I'm not alone. I have AI."

**Key insight:** "I'm not 'just one person.' I'm one person with AI leverage, modern infrastructure, and 15+ years of development experience."

**Transition:** "But let's be specific about what this means for you as a potential customer."

## 3. The Advantages You Get (500 words)

**Thesis:** Solo operation provides specific benefits you won't get from larger companies.

### 3.1 Speed to Ship

- No committee decisions or approval chains
- Feature requests → implementation in days, not quarters
- Example: LinkedIn Ghostwriter feature iteration cycle
- Direct feedback loop: you talk to me, I build it

### 3.2 Direct Access to the Builder

- No support tier 1, 2, 3 escalation
- Talk directly to the person who built the system
- I understand the architecture, the decisions, the tradeoffs
- Your feedback shapes the product directly

### 3.3 Aligned Incentives

- No VC pressure to grow fast and burn out
- No pressure to pivot or sell
- My success = your success (product quality, not user count)
- Built for sustainability, not quick exit

### 3.4 Focus on Product Quality

- Not distracted by team management, politics, or fundraising
- 100% of my time goes to building and supporting
- Every line of code, every decision is mine
- Deep ownership of quality

### 3.5 Cost Efficiency

- Lower overhead = sustainable pricing
- No need to support large team salaries
- Savings passed to customers (competitive pricing)
- Can serve niche markets profitably

**Real example:** "When you email me about a bug, I'm the one who wrote that code. I can fix it today, not next sprint."

**Transition:** "Of course, solo operation has legitimate concerns. Here's how I address them."

## 4. The Risks I'm Mitigating (600 words)

**Thesis:** I'm aware of the risks and have specific strategies to address each one.

### 4.1 The "Bus Factor" Problem

**Concern:** What if you get hit by a bus?

**Mitigation:**

- Comprehensive documentation (architecture, deployment, operations)
- Code organization following industry standards
- Infrastructure as Code (reproducible environments)
- Succession plan documented
- Open roadmap (customers know what's coming)

**Reality:** Most startups have bus factor of 1-2 anyway. The difference is I'm transparent about it.

### 4.2 Support Availability

**Concern:** Can one person handle support 24/7?

**Mitigation:**

- Clear SLAs published upfront
- AI-assisted support for common issues
- Automated monitoring and alerting
- Response time commitments (and I meet them)
- Status page for transparency

**Current metrics:** (Will add real data once live)

- Average response time: X hours
- Resolution time: Y hours
- Uptime: 99.X%

### 4.3 Product Longevity

**Concern:** Will this product exist in a year?

**Mitigation:**

- This is my full-time focus (not a side project)
- Financial runway planned for 18+ months
- Sustainable business model (not burn-and-hope)
- Open roadmap showing commitment
- Regular build log updates (transparency)

**Proof points:**

- Full-time commitment since [date]
- Registered business entity
- Professional infrastructure
- Long-term vision documented

### 4.4 Quality and Reliability

**Concern:** Can one person maintain production quality?

**Mitigation:**

- Production-grade infrastructure from day one
- Comprehensive testing (unit, integration, E2E)
- Observability and monitoring
- Security best practices
- Regular audits and updates

**My background:**

- 15+ years full-stack development
- Built and operated production systems at scale
- Deep expertise in AWS/CDK infrastructure
- Methodology documented and proven

### 4.5 Scaling Challenges

**Concern:** What happens when the product grows?

**Mitigation:**

- Built for scale from the start (cloud-native, serverless)
- Automation handles growth (not manual processes)
- Clear thresholds for when to hire
- Not opposed to growing team when it makes sense

**Where solo struggles (and my plan):**

- Enterprise sales → Target SMBs and indie builders first
- Complex integrations → Focus on focused, standalone products
- High-touch support → Build for self-service, clear documentation
- Regulated industries → Start with less regulated markets

**Key insight:** "I'm aware of the limitations. I'm building for markets where solo is an advantage, and I have a plan for when it's not."

## 5. What This Means for You (400 words)

**Thesis:** Here's how to evaluate solo-built products and what to expect.

### 5.1 If You're a Potential Customer

**What to expect:**

- Fast iteration and direct communication
- Transparent roadmap and honest limitations
- Quality product built by someone who cares
- Sustainable pricing and long-term focus

**What NOT to expect:**

- 24/7 phone support
- Enterprise sales process
- Dedicated account manager
- Instant responses at 3 AM

**How to evaluate:**

- Judge the product quality, not team size
- Check the documentation and infrastructure
- Look at response times and transparency
- Try it—most products have free trials

**Green flags:**

- Professional infrastructure (status page, monitoring)
- Clear SLAs and communication
- Regular updates and transparency
- Strong technical depth
- Sustainable business model

**Red flags:**

- No documentation or unclear processes
- Vague about availability or support
- Side project energy (irregular updates)
- No clear business model
- Defensive about being solo

### 5.2 If You're a Builder Considering Solo

**When solo makes sense:**

- You have deep technical expertise
- You're targeting niche markets
- You value speed and autonomy
- You can leverage AI effectively
- You're building focused products

**When you need a team:**

- Enterprise sales required
- Multiple specialized skill sets needed
- 24/7 coverage is critical
- Regulated industry with compliance needs
- Complex integrations with many partners

**Key insight:** "Solo isn't for everyone or every product. But for the right market and the right builder, it's incredibly powerful."

**Transition:** "Here's my commitment to you."
