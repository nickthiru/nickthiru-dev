_**Claude Opus 4.6**_:  
Yes, this is absolutely standard and good practice. Many successful bloggers, indie makers, and technical writers do exactly this — pin their highest-signal content so it doesn't get buried under a chronological feed. You're right to think about it proactively before you have 30+ articles.

## Naming options

"Must Read" works but can feel slightly presumptuous depending on your audience. Here are alternatives ranked roughly by how well they'd fit your site's tone (professional, understated, confident but not flashy):

| Term                  | Vibe                                                               |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Start Here**        | Welcoming, great for new visitors. Very common on creator sites.   |
| **Featured**          | Clean, neutral, professional.                                      |
| **Essential Reading** | Authoritative without being pushy.                                 |
| **Foundations**       | Fits well if the articles represent your core philosophy/approach. |
| **Pinned**            | Familiar from social platforms, self-explanatory.                  |
| **Core Ideas**        | Intellectual, signals these represent your thinking.               |
| **Must Read**         | Direct, works if your brand leans confident.                       |

Given that your site tagline is "Building agentic AI systems that ship" and your writing categories are engineering/business/product, I'd lean toward **"Start Here"** or **"Foundations"** — especially if those three articles represent _why_ someone should care about your perspective (like the "Why I Build Production AI Systems with Code, Not Drag-and-Drop" piece, which is clearly a differentiator).

## Where to place it

You have two good options:

**1. On the homepage, between the hero and "Recent Writing"**
This is the highest-visibility spot. A small section with 3 cards (or even a more compact horizontal row) labeled "Start Here" before the chronological feed. This way every visitor sees them regardless of whether they click into /writing.

**2. On the /writing page, above the filtered article list**
Right below the three-category explainer cards and above the "All / Engineering / Business / Product" filter tabs. This keeps /writing as the canonical place for all content, with the featured pieces getting prime real estate.

I'd actually suggest **both** — a compact version on the homepage (maybe just titles + one-line descriptions, no tags/metadata) and the full cards on /writing. The homepage version acts as a hook; the /writing version gives them the full context.

## One design consideration

Since you currently have a nice visual rhythm — hero → recent writing on the homepage, and category cards → filter tabs → articles on /writing — make sure the featured section is visually distinct enough that it doesn't just look like more article cards. A subtle background color, a slightly different card style, or even just a thin top border accent could work. You already use the light purple/lavender background for sections, so you have a design language for this.

Three articles is a great number for this. Enough to be substantive, few enough to not dilute the signal. Just revisit the selection every 6–12 months to make sure it still represents your best differentiating work.

_**Claude Opus 4.6**_:  
Good questions all around. Let me take them one at a time.

## Keep "Recent Writing" or replace it?

I'd **replace it** on the homepage. Here's the reasoning:

- You only have four articles right now, and three of them are the ones you want to pin. Showing a "Recent Writing" section underneath would largely duplicate the same content.
- Even as your article count grows, the homepage doesn't need to be a feed. That's what /writing is for. The homepage should be curated — your best foot forward.
- The "View all →" link on the pinned section already gives visitors a clear path to the full archive.

So the homepage flow becomes: **Hero → Pinned articles → Subscribe → Current projects**. Clean, intentional, no redundancy.

One caveat: once you have, say, 15+ articles, you _might_ want to bring back a small "Latest" row (2–3 articles) below the pinned section so repeat visitors can quickly see what's new without clicking through. But that's a future problem.

## Section title

Given that your other sections are "Current projects" and you want consistency, here are options:

| Option                | Fit                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Essential reading** | Pairs well with "Current projects" — both sound curated and intentional. My top pick. |
| **Start here**        | Slightly too casual alongside "Current projects."                                     |
| **Featured writing**  | Works but sounds a bit like a magazine.                                               |
| **Foundations**       | Strong but might imply the articles are introductory/basic.                           |
| **Core writing**      | Decent, but "core" is vague.                                                          |

**"Essential reading"** works best because:

- "Current projects" says _here's what I'm building now_
- "Essential reading" says _here's what you should read first_
- Both imply curation — one by recency, one by significance

Use the same title on the /writing page for consistency. Visitors who saw it on the homepage will recognize it immediately.

## Card design

Yes, **strip them down**. For the pinned articles, you want them to feel like recommendations, not feed items. Title + a 2-line synopsis is exactly right. Removing the category badge, date, reading time, and tags does two things:

1. **Visually distinguishes** them from the regular article cards so they clearly belong to a different section.
2. **Makes them feel timeless.** The moment you put a date on a pinned article, it starts aging. "Jan 9, 2026" will look stale by mid-year. These are meant to be evergreen — the card design should reinforce that.

For the **/writing page**, I'd use the **same minimal card design** for the pinned section there too. The contrast between the clean pinned cards at the top and the full-detail cards in the filtered list below will naturally create visual hierarchy — the pinned section feels curated and elevated, the list below feels like the working archive. That distinction does the work for you without needing extra styling.

So to summarize the final structure:

**Homepage:**

> Hero → **Essential reading** (3 minimal cards + "View all →") → Subscribe → Current projects

**/writing page:**

> Intro + category cards → **Essential reading** (3 minimal cards) → Filter tabs → Article list (full cards)
